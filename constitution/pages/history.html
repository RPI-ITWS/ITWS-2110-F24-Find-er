Constitutional History

  • Articles of Confederation (1777-1781)

    The Articles of Confederation was the first governing document of the United States after declaring independence, ratified in 1781. This was viewed as weak, due to delegating most power to the states.

  • Constitutional Convention (1787)

    The Constitutional Convention was a meeting of delegates from the states, where they met in Philadelphia to draft a new Constitution. This was specifically held due to the failure of the Articles of Confederation.

  • Virginia Plan vs. New Jersey Plan (1787)

    The Virginia and New Jersey Plans were at the forefront of debates on Representation in Congress at the Constitutional Convention. The Virginia Plan favored the larger states, as representation was proportional to population, and the New Jersey Plan favored the smaller states, with equal representation among states.

  • The Great Compromise (1787)

    The Great Compromise combined elements of both the Virginia Plan and the New Jersey Plan by establishing a bicameral legislature, or a two house congress. This bicameral legislature contains the Senate, which has equal representation among states, and the House of Representatives which has representation based on population.

  • Three-Fifths Compromise (1787)

    The Three-Fifths Compromise was a controversial agreement, which decided that three-fifths of slaves would count towards state representation and taxation.

  • The Federalist and Anti-Federalist Papers (1787-1788)

    The Federalist Papers were a collection of 85 essays written by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay Under the Pseudonym, “Publius” These Papers advocated for a strong central government and the ratification of the constitution. The Anti-Federalist Papers were in response to the Federalist Papers, showing concern of the central government becoming too powerful, and was against the ratification of the constitution.

  • Ratification of the Constitution (1787-1788)

    After being written in 1787, the Draft of the Constitution was sent to the states for approval. After push from the federalists, it was ratified in 1788.

  • Judiciary Act of 1789 (1789)

    Not long after the ratification of the constitution, this act established the federal judiciary system which was the structure for the supreme court among other courts. This also gave the judiciary the ability to interpret laws and ensure they are constitutional.

  • The Bill of Rights (1791)

    After the Constitution was ratified, there was a concern about the protection of individual liberties. This led to the addition of the bill of rights, which were also the first ten amendments to the constitution. The Bill of Rights was able to guarantee the individual rights of the American people, such as freedom of speech, religion, and due process.

  • Early Constitutional Changes (1790s–1800s)

    • 11th Amendment (1795)
    • 12th Amendment (1804)

    These early amendments addressed issues related to states' sovereign immunity and the electoral process for choosing the President and Vice President.

  • Marbury v. Madison (1803)

    This landmark supreme court case led to the establishment of judicial review. Judicial review is given the court's power to nullify any laws that they deem unconstitutional. This contributed to the system used for checks and balances.

  • McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)

    This was an important case in upholding the principle of implied powers under the constitution. This also affirmed the government’s ability to create a national bank, and ruled that states could not tax federal institutions. This further strengthened federal authority.
